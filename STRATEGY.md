# Cybersecurity Community — Strategy & Scaling Plan

_Last updated: 2026-05-22_

## 1. Context & objective

We are building a community for cybersecurity professionals and closely related
engineering roles in the Netherlands. The community is sponsored by an HR
solo-founder (based in the Netherlands) who specialises in recruiting and placing:

- Cybersecurity developers / security engineers
- Cybersecurity professionals (broadly)
- Cloud engineers
- Linux / infrastructure engineers

The business objective: in **6–9 months**, give the sponsor a warm, trusted place
to source and place these professionals. Design inspiration: **dpo-club.org**.

**Reframed honestly, this is a community-led talent network** — a genuine
professional community whose downstream benefit is high-trust recruiting. The
order matters: community value first, recruiting second. A community built
primarily as a recruiting funnel will not survive contact with this audience.

## 2. The honest strategic read

- **dpo-club.org is an India-focused Data Protection Officer community.** What is
  worth copying is the *model*, not the audience: community + city chapters +
  flagship events + advisory board + a **separate placement arm**
  (dpoassociation.org does the hiring; the club stays neutral). Copy the brand
  separation — the community must look neutral; the sponsor's HR firm is a
  "partner," not the owner.
- **"DPO Club" works because "DPO" is one defined statutory role.**
  Cybersecurity has no single clean role, so naming after "the people" is harder
  — favour a topic/identity name (see §6).
- **Cybersecurity people are the most skeptical audience for "community-washing."**
  Optimise the community for trust; let placements be the byproduct.
- **Timeline reality:** 6–9 months to "a small, trusted community + first real
  placements" is achievable. "Scaled" is an 18–24 month story.
- **Non-negotiable:** a Dutch HR founder cannot be the face of a cyber community
  alone. The first move is to recruit a **credible cyber-native practitioner**
  as co-host / community lead.

## 3. Competitive landscape — Netherlands

This is a crowded, mature market. A generic "cybersecurity community" has no pull.

| Player | What it is | Audience | What it leaves open |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OWASP Netherlands** | Free appsec meetups (Amsterdam / Utrecht) | Technical / appsec / devs | No career/hiring focus; appsec-narrow; uncurated |
| **BSides Amsterdam** | Community hacker conference | Deep-technical hackers | Annual, large, not intimate or career-oriented |
| **OrangeCon / ONE Conference** | Non-profit / govt-business conferences | Practitioners; senior/policy | Once-a-year events, not an ongoing community |
| **CISO Community Nederland** | CISO peer network + CISODAY | C-level only | Excludes everyone below CISO |
| **PvIB** | Established Dutch infosec platform | Broad, Dutch-language, formal | Older/formal feel; not intimate or career-stage aware |
| **IAPP KnowledgeNet NL** | Privacy / AI-governance law | Privacy & legal | Privacy-only |
| **DIVD** | Volunteer vulnerability disclosure | Researchers | Mission-specific, not networking/career |
| **Polder Security (Stichting)** | NL non-profit running community infosec events; organises BSides Amsterdam | Hackers, students, builders | Event/CTF-led, volunteer-run; not a curated career network |
| **The Engineers Club** | Online community + courses — cloud security, DevSecOps, platform engineering | Security/infra engineers (global) | Course-led and global/online; not NL-local or intimate-events-led |
| **Benelux Cyber Summit / Cybersec NL** | Commercial summits | Vendors + buyers | Paid, vendor-heavy |

**The gap nobody owns:** an *ongoing, small, curated, career-and-peer-focused*
community for the **engineers who build, secure and operate modern
infrastructure** — security engineers, cloud engineers, Linux/platform engineers,
and security-minded developers. Everyone else is deep-technical-appsec
(OWASP/BSides), senior-only (CISO Community), event-only (the conferences), or
formal/old (PvIB). **The Engineers Club** proves there is demand for exactly this
wedge — but it is global and course-led; the **NL-local, intimate, curated,
career-focused** version of it is still wide open.

## 4. Geographic read — the Netherlands market

**Why the timing is good:**

- ~**13,800 open cybersecurity roles** in NL in 2026; average **127 days to fill**
  vs. 82 days for general IT.
- **NIS2** is forcing **~89% of Dutch firms** to hire extra security staff.
- In-demand skills: **cloud security, identity & access management (IAM),
  Zero Trust architecture, AI-aware security** — heavily engineering/infra,
  which maps directly to the sponsor's placement specialties.
- Salaries: €42k (entry) → €120k (senior). High enough that career advancement is
  a real reason to join a community.

**The catch — GDPR / AVG.** We are collecting cybersecurity professionals into a
database to introduce them to employers. This audience will judge us on our own
data practices. Requirements: explicit **separate opt-in consent** for
"community membership" vs. "share my profile with hiring partners," a clean
privacy notice, and proper processing registration for the HR firm. Done well,
this is a trust signal; done sloppily, it is fatal.

## 5. The wedge — who this community is for

A community must align with what the sponsor can actually **place**. Her book is
engineering-heavy (security devs, cloud, Linux), and NL demand is engineering-heavy
(cloud security, IAM, Zero Trust). The two line up cleanly.

**Recommended wedge: "secure systems engineering" — the people who build, secure
and run modern infrastructure in the Netherlands.**

Concentric circles:

- **Core:** security engineers, DevSecOps, cloud security engineers, appsec /
  security-minded developers.
- **Ring 1 (the differentiator):** cloud engineers, Linux / platform / SRE /
  infrastructure engineers — security-adjacent builders. No community owns them.
- **Ring 2 (recruiting supply):** security-curious infrastructure engineers and
  career-changers moving toward security — the easiest to place, served by nobody.

Why this wins: it is simultaneously the **empty market niche**, a **real identity**
people self-apply ("platform engineer," "DevSecOps," "cloud security"), and the
**most placeable talent pool** for the sponsor's business.

> Open question to validate: whether to keep the framing tightly engineering
> ("secure systems / cloud / infra") or slightly broader ("cybersecurity +
> the engineers around it"). This should be pressure-tested against live NL job
> demand and the sponsor's actual current mandates before launch.

## 6. Naming

**Working name: Vauntio.**

The naming process tested and rejected several directions:

- **Role-based names** (e.g. "CISO Club") — ruled out; they lock the community to
  one seniority and break the cross-role wedge.
- **Descriptive / metaphor names** — exhausted in this category. "The Cyber
  Guild," "Cyber Yard," "Polder," "Engineers Club," "Bastion" and "CloudArmor"
  are all already taken by cybersecurity organisations or products — some are
  direct lookalikes (see §3).
- **Semi-meaningful coined names** (Vallum, Munio, Securo, Castrum, Cybral) —
  every one is already a registered security company.

The decision: a **fully-coined, brandable name**. **Vauntio**, verified clean on
2026-05-22:

- No existing company or product namesake found.
- `vauntio.com`, `vauntio.io` and `vauntio.nl` all unregistered and available.

Trade-off accepted: a coined name carries no inherent meaning, so it **must
always travel with a descriptive tagline** — e.g. _"Vauntio — for the engineers
who build, secure and run modern infrastructure in the Netherlands."_

Before committing:

- [ ] Formal trademark search (Benelux BOIP and EUIPO registers).
- [ ] Register `vauntio.com` + `.io` + `.nl` and secure the LinkedIn page and
      social handles the same day, to prevent squatting.
- [ ] Lock the tagline alongside the name.

## 7. Scaling plan

This maps to the sponsor's own idea: small group → 5–10 person events → a 20–30
person event → scale.

| Phase | Timeline | Community | Format | Recruiting | Website |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **0 — Foundation** | Month 1 | Recruit cyber-native co-host. Hand-pick 8–12 "founding circle" via 1:1 outreach. | — | None. Set up GDPR-clean intake (separate consents). | One-page landing + waitlist. |
| **1 — Intimate proof** | Months 2–3 | Grow to 30–50 in chat. | 1 online roundtable/month + 1 offline coffee/dinner. **5–10 people**, no slides, Chatham House rule, one real shared problem. | None overt. Observe who is strong / who is open. Soft "career corner." | + events page + apply form. |
| **2 — Signature event** | Months 4–6 | 80–150 members. | First **20–30 person** offline evening (talk + roundtables + drinks), venue sponsored. | Introduce **opt-in job board / "who's hiring / who's looking"** channel. First placements realistic Month 5–7. | + opt-in job board + opt-in member directory. |
| **3 — Repeatable engine** | Months 7–12 | 200–400 members; possible 2nd city chapter. | Monthly small + quarterly flagship. | Hiring-partner subscriptions; sponsorships become a revenue line. | Mature platform. |
| **4 — Scale** | Year 2+ | Multi-city / Benelux; consider a training or certification track. | — | Recurring revenue. | — |

**The format that wins** (and is the moat): small, *no-slides*, peer roundtables
on a real shared pain — e.g. "what your cloud security tooling actually costs,"
"running Linux fleets securely at scale," "breaking into security from infra."
Curated invites, Chatham House rule. The big meetups and conferences cannot offer
this intimacy.

**Per-phase metrics:** "would you recommend?" score, event attendance &
repeat-rate, chat weekly-active users, join applications, **% who opt into the
recruiting layer**, intros made, placements.

## 8. The website

Do **not** build a platform for a 12-person community. Build only what each phase
needs.

- **Phase 0–1:** one static landing page (Astro or Next.js, deployed on Vercel) —
  who / what / why + waitlist form. Use **Luma** for events (RSVPs, reminders,
  pages — do not build this). Community home: Slack or Discord; a LinkedIn page
  for visibility.
- **Phase 2:** add events page + member application + an opt-in job board.
- **Phase 3:** member directory, newsletter, recap content.

The natural first build in this repository is the Phase-0 landing page, structured
like dpo-club.org (hero → who it's for → events → join), minus the certification
content.

## 9. Risks & how to de-risk

1. **Perceived as a recruiter's funnel** → brand separation (club neutral, HR firm
   = "partner"); recruiting strictly opt-in and downstream.
2. **No cyber credibility** → recruit a respected practitioner co-host *first*.
3. **Crowded market** → compete on intimacy, the secure-systems-engineering
   identity, and career progression — not on events.
4. **GDPR misstep in front of privacy-aware professionals** → separate consents,
   clean privacy notice, proper registration — treat compliance as a feature.
5. **Founder burnout / slow trust** → keep Phase 0–1 deliberately small; do not
   scale before the format is loved.

## 10. Next 90 days

- **Weeks 1–2:** Lock the wedge, name and domain. Recruit the cyber-native
  co-host. Draft positioning.
- **Weeks 3–4:** Build the one-page landing + waitlist. Stand up Slack/Discord +
  LinkedIn. Hand-pick and personally invite 8–12 founding members.
- **Weeks 5–12:** Run 2 online roundtables + 1 offline meetup. Refine the format.
  Reach 30–50 engaged members. Quietly map who is strong and who is open to moving.
